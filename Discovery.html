Origin of Quinine

Quinine was used in the early 1600s by Jesuit missionaries in South America. It was not the discovery of the isolation of quinine but the use of cinchona tree bark in their medicinal practices.

Spread to America

The isolation of quinine, first discovered in 1820 by two French chemists, Pelletier and Caventou, in 1820 marked the transition from herbs and plant extracts to modern pharmaceuticals. American doctors reacted to the discovery by offering this isolated quinine as standard treatment among patients.

Misconception in Medicine

Before the Germ Theory, Americans previously believed that malaria was caused by bad air and swamps. This belief is recognized as the miasma theory. Doctors understood that quinine worked as it cured symptoms of malaria and fevers but as years of war come to an end, the same doctors begin to question the belief which lead to the development of a new theory into modern medical understanding. Quinine worked even though the theory was wrong because quinine killed the Plasmodium parasites in malaria.
