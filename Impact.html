Global Trade

The demand for quinine increased global trade in the United States and contributed to the growth of the global pharmaceutical market. However, this trade also created conflicts with several countries, especially the Dutch and other European powers, who controlled much of the quinine supply.

Effect of Supply Disruptions

Price changes or shortages of quinine negatively affected doctors. Malaria and other diseases were very prevalent in the West and South. For example, during the Civil War, blockages of quinine supply in the South caused many doctors to panic in trying to find alternatives to quinine. These delays led to many patients receiving inadequate care and exposed them to traditional means of treatment.

Impact on Agriculture and Settlement

When settlers were moving west, they were faced with many challenges in settling in a specific area. One of these challenges was settling in towns near rivers that were prone to malaria breakouts, but with the introduction of quinine, towns near rivers, and many other malaria-free areas were open to settlement as it became more habitable after the widespread use of quinine in medical practices. This allowed for more territory to be used by settlers,s addressing challenges with migration patterns and, in return, further encouraging agricultural development.

Unfair Distribution of Quinine

In North America (primarily the West Coast), Chinese immigrants began arriving in significant numbers in the 1850s. Their access to Western-style medicine, like quinine, was limited, as they often relied on traditional Chinese medicine, and they frequently faced discrimination that restricted access to mainstream medical supplies.

Native American Access to Quinine

While Native Americans in South America were the original discoverers of the medicinal properties of cinchona bark, in North America, access to refined quinine between 1800 and 1860 was limited. However, some Native American communities may have had access to various forms of bark or traditional, non-quinine remedies, or obtained them through trade or early interactions with government agents; though they generally lacked the systematic, high-volume access enjoyed by European Americans.
